How to use this dashboard

Search by audit type, framework, evidence, red flag or use case. Open a card to view scope, testing, evidence, deliverables and source anchors. Select up to three cards for comparison. After comparing, use Reset comparison to clear selected cards. This is a practical, non-exhaustive taxonomy; sector, regulator and organisation-specific audits can be added over time.

Audit universe

The audit universe is the complete, living inventory of auditable areas that could be reviewed by internal audit or other assurance providers. It normally includes entities, processes, systems, products, programmes, locations, vendors, data assets, reports, regulations, models and major risk themes.

Use this tab to move from a list of audit disciplines into a risk-based planning view: what can be audited, who owns it, how risky it is, what assurance already exists and what should be prioritised in the annual or rolling audit plan.

Scope sufficiency checklist

      
The practical audit scope shown in each discipline is suitable as a starting scope universe. Before formal use, convert it into an approved engagement scope by confirming the boundary, criteria, period, systems, locations, data population, exclusions, stakeholders, reliance and reporting expectations.

      

        
1. Boundary
  • Business process, product, service, entity or system covered
  • In-scope locations, teams, outsourced providers and interfaces
  • Period under review and cut-off date

        
2. Criteria
  • Policies, procedures, laws, standards or control framework used
  • Risk appetite, KPIs, SLAs, thresholds and management commitments
  • Clear basis for rating design and operating effectiveness

        
3. Evidence and data
  • Population owner, source system, completeness check and retention period
  • Evidence quality: sufficient, reliable, relevant and useful
  • Data limitations, alternative evidence and reliance on other assurance providers

        
4. Exclusions
  • Explicit out-of-scope areas and why they are excluded
  • Known limitations such as unavailable data or legal restrictions
  • Items deferred to another audit, regulator, external assurance or management review

      

      
Verdict: the Hub’s practical scope is sufficient for an explorer and planning aid. For a formal engagement, the generator now adds a deeper scope boundary checklist and out-of-scope section.

Credibility guardrails for factual and defensible use

      
These guardrails keep the hub credible. They separate globally recognised references from local or sector-specific applicability, and they prevent the dashboard from appearing to issue audit opinions without evidence.

    

    

      
1. No unsourced maturity score
Do not display maturity percentages unless a formal assessment has been completed with approved criteria and evidence. This version uses qualitative indicators instead.

      
2. Source before assertion
Every framework named in the dashboard is mapped to an official or recognised public source. The source register shows what each source supports.

      
3. Local applicability check
Jurisdiction-specific references such as privacy law, cyber law, sustainability reporting rules and sector circulars should be applied only where they are relevant to the organisation’s legal, policy or sector context.

      
4. Evidence-based closure
Audit issues should not be closed based on management statement alone. Closure should require documentation, sample evidence, system logs or observable control operation.

      
5. Avoid duplicate assurance
Before launching a review, check whether another function already tests the same controls. Use the assurance map to coordinate internal audit, compliance, risk, technology and management testing.

      
6. Board-ready language
Committee reporting should focus on root causes, recurring themes, critical overdue actions, strategic impact and public value not only issue counts.
